This is the day after watching The Odyssey as visualized by Christopher Nolan, and I have sensed my perspectives on the film mature and change over the last 24 hours, so I thought I would pen them down as they are. I warn all readers that there are spoilers in the paragraphs to come.

## REVIEW 

Firstly, getting it out of the way, since this review is focused on the thematic narrative, everything else about the film is brilliantly done, the colors, the lighting, the scale and awe, the presence, very Nolan-esque, and an absolute delight to consume. 10/10 on those aspects.

Focusing on the monsters. I thoroughly enjoyed the character design of Polyphemus, because any Greek mythology afficionado is likely to have come across depictions of Cyclops and one-eyed monsters, rendering their unnatural ambience as merely something odd or a bit weird. But Nolan’s silent mishapen Polyphemus that dismisses the soldiers is deeply alien and unnerving that fits right in with the grand epic on screen.

That takes me directly to my favorite depiction of Greek mythology so far, the unassuming, ordinary woman, surrounded by docile wild animals, in her home at the top, Circe. Despite how much lore I had consumed, and the animals being a dead giveaway for any mythology fan, in my heart, I still wondered, is this really Circe? Not because Nolan had depicted her poorly, but he had done it brilliantly. She’s a commoner begrudgingly serving her guests, until she’s not. 

And then she molds them into their true forms. She pulls and stretches and shapes, it is painful, it is horror, you look away, but you cannot look away. By far, an immense highlight of the three hours. Even in the final moments, we get to see Circe, and we are left with a clever line, “Back, back to your disguises.” (I eagerly await when the script becomes available.)

Scylla and Charybdis might have been where the film falls short a bit, but the character design of Scylla was still beautiful to watch, where it felt like, briefly, even the mountains had come to life and turned against them out of pure spite.

The Laestrygonians provided a remarkable presence, despite following so close after Polyphemus, a much bigger giant. From the introduction of the eerie child that stands taller than Odysseus, to the giant warriors in their white-grey metal armor, marching inevitably like a force of nature, you understand why everyone would naturally turn and flee.

All in all, Nolan’s Greek monsters and myths were wonderfully wrought in all of their horror, that I deeply desire his next venture is in the horror genre itself. While the time he spends on each of them in isolation is minimal, I am certain the depictions will stand strong for decades to come.

When it comes to the narrative, both structure and theme, the film shares a very common thread with its predecessor in Nolan’s endeavours, Oppenheimer. Both detail the troubled conscience of men as they conceive of something clever, rally a crowd to get it going, and as they observe the consequences, proceed to understand that they have unleashed an immoral primal urge upon their race.

This is what I come to odds at, that thematically all this while, to me, Homer’s Odyssey, in the forms I know it, is of the yearning and perseverance to return home. I confess now that this might have been a childlike understanding of the literature based on what I have been reading in the last many hours. Nevertheless, while I enjoyed the scenes of Nolan’s Odyssey individually, I was confused at how they meshed together narratively for the first two acts of the movie. At that point, it wasn’t my favorite Nolan film and I was going to rate it three or four stars.

The third act changed how I perceived it looking back, the specific subset of monsters chosen, their depictions, and the common thread that unites them; Zeus’ Law. This abstract object that sits as the large backdrop changed how I looked back on the film, how I desire to intensely to rewatch it under the new lens it presents. Now, I might even consider it a perfect film.

## ANALYSIS

Nolan’s Odyssey chronicles the beginning of a culture of dishonor and the men who start it, Odysseus, Menelaus, Agamemnon, who stand appalled as they have constructed a world they no longer fit in. These three men are highlighted as they are used by Nolan to demonstrate they now come from a different era of societal respect that no longer exists, one that they unknowingly served to destabilize through a carefully crafted massacre.

Menelaus is the sole good host, evidenced by his actions for Telemachus, where he hunts with him and feeds him, which lines up with Menelaus being a man out of time with the company he now finds himself in. But he preserves his respect; he demands to meet all strangers himself. Agamemnon, despite his brutality, suspects nothing but a glorious welcome on his return home and sheds all of his layers of armor to be stabbed. Odysseus, despite being warned and provided with knowledge, still asks in disguise if there aren’t men who would take care of Odysseus’ son and wife. These men are learning slowly of the consequence of their action and are regretful too late, at different stages in their understanding.

This theme, as some folks have pointed out on Twitter, also runs rampant through the different monsters chosen who play the role of bad hosts, such as Polyphemus, Calypso, Circe, and the Laestrygonians. Of these, the closest to kindness portrayed by Nolan is Calypso, who is the singular character separated from humanity, unaware of the birth of the men of the sea and the culture they bring with them.

Through the monsters, Odysseus and his men go through repeated iterations of what they put Troy through. They enter a comforting environment (Polyphemus’s sheep and cheese, Circe’s food, Calypso’s comfort) and believe Zeus’ law will be practiced and safeguard them. This is the Trojan horse they accept as they now play their roles as the people of Troy. 

And in their vulnerability and comfort, the aggressors (the violent consumption of Polyphemus, the witchcraft of Circe, the time stolen by Calypso) reactively take the stage to play the role of Odysseus. Through their interactions with a monster, a witch, a goddess-nymph, this wide array of non-human interactions, Odysseus and his men are forcefully fed the truth that they now belong amongst those whom they repulse. They can no longer go back, no matter how many times they invoke and expect Zeus’ law.

Their humanity was sacrificed once the first sword was drawn out of the horse.

Parallely, word has spread of the Trojan war, and a culture of distrust and dishonor has permeated the world, which gives rise to Nolan’s repeated mention of “the people of the sea”. While we repeatedly hear them referenced, we discover this culture was birthed by Odysseus in his confession to Penelope. “To burn the walls of Troy was to burn the world entire.”

Another idea that runs in tandem with this interpretation of Nolan’s Odyssey is the representation of the Gods. We are never shown them except for apparently Athena (portrayed by Zendaya). Poseidon and Zeus are repeatedly mentioned but never portrayed on screen. This confused me until the third act reveals Athena has taken the form of a young girl ruthlessly slain in the Trojan War. 

So, as many have begun suspecting, do the Gods exist in this Odyssey? Is what we have perceived as Athena merely a hallucination of Odysseus’s conscience? Or does the goddess choose to manifest as his conscience? The latter is what I lean towards, where Nolan’s Greek Gods are humanity’s projections onto the world around them. To me, in this retelling, the gods manifest as different forms of destiny, Athena as his conscience, and Poseidon as the seas and tides. Both shaping the Odyssey.

But what about Zeus? We hear bursts of thunder and lightning, but I consider that hardly impactful to the tale from a god that has haunted the narrative. Instead, as I have presented the case that Nolan portrays Odysseus as having sunk to the stature of a monster with his violation, I ask, can the pendulum swing the other way?

As Odysseus confronts Antinous at the banquet, taking on the form of a beggar and Sinon, he lies in a state of flux. He is not worthy to reclaim his role of the king of Ithaca, as his soul lies shapeless, in tattered clothes that merely enclose the shape of a man. Zeus’ Law enters again through Antinous’ outburst, and the arrogant suitor flings a question at the beggar. Is it he, the king of the gods, in disguise? We’ve arrived at the beginning of Odysseus’ final mutation at the end of the Odyssey.

When the suitors fail at Penelope’s challenge, Antinous, in apparent resignation, deems the task as “impossible”, implying it cannot be achieved by a mortal. The beggar silently steps forward, claiming his turn, and Odysseus rises to the task. Once he strings the bow and completes the challenge, he re-orients the bow to those who have violated Zeus’ law and snark at it. Now, Odysseus has begun his ascension as Zeus, bringing righteous punishment.

Where does lightning come from? Above. As Odysseus swings around with arrows embedded in him, beyond mortal tolerance, where do his weapons of attack come from when the bow isn’t enough? Above, the hole in the ceiling, where Telemachus delivers the swords at the pivotal moment in the fight, a Nolan-addition to the tale. Zeus thus completes his manifestation in the epic as Odysseus himself.

This is my interpretation of Nolan’s Odyssey thus far, of Gods and Monsters, where Odysseus transforms between both. Nolan views this epic and Odysseus’ actions as an immense dismantling of a civilized peaceful society with a treacherous act. Through his epic, he seeks to illustrate that we are Polyphemus, we are Circe, we are Calypso, but if we come to a civilized understanding, we can pave the way to a sustainable culture, allowing us to rise to the nature of the divine.
