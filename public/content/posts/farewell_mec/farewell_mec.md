In two days, I have a farewell party and I was perplexed about what to wear.

And then it hit me.

The lovely farewell coat that I wore at my last farewell, i.e, in 12th grade.

As I admired my reflection in the smooth shiny outfit in the mirror, I began to get lost in my thoughts. The teen who wore this in 12th grade, and the young adult wearing it now, were entirely different individuals. It’s a cinematic cliche to say of course, but some cliches are inspired from real life, in the end.

![The first photo I took as an MECian. Those teeth, just wow.](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*NqmrlXSDpS-xGkJ4w7ba0Q.jpeg)

> I spent the last three years showcasing the best feats of writing possible on Medium here, and every article has been an attempt to connect with a larger crowd who hopefully felt the same way I did, and every story a chance to blow you away with a twist.
> 
> This entry is neither of those things.
> 
> This is me saying goodbye to an energetic ecosystem of people and events that kept me on my feet, grabbed me by my spine until I was a fish out of water, spluttering for breath, and at the end of it all, I had lost almost all the hair on my head and gained the compensatory amount on my face.

I doubt this entry connects because this is writing infused with the energy of a boy who came from a different country four years ago, walked into a building that looked like a malnutritioned plant living on borrowed time, and when I peered into my classroom, I saw an entirely different culture than I had been used to for the last 18 years of my life.

A language whose slang I wasn’t familiar with, inside jokes I could not comprehend, random catcall-like “Poli Sharathe, track maat!” yells, excuse me, sir, what now, and so on. I knew Malayalam but I used all the wrong words, romance wasn’t _premam_, sadness wasn’t _dukham,_ and to date, I am still updating my vocabulary.

I detested almost all of the first year because honestly, the subjects were terrible. When I wrote Chemistry in the Board Exams, I dreamed of how my Bachelors degree in Computer Science, i.e, the national sport, would never bring me face to face with this subject again. One can dream and have those hopes dashed against walls (that desperately need repainting).

I promised myself repeatedly if I got a break, I would tidy up my room and complete all overdue work, and explore everything I was interested in.

I got my wishes granted when the college announced a two-week leave. My much-needed break had arrived.

Apparently, there was some virus that affected three people in Kochi or something.

---

My first semester marks had arrived. They were horrific. Being an engineer was clearly not going to pan out. I needed an alternate mode/backup plan to ensure I land on my two feet.

Time to give writing a shot, the dream I had always had but barely ever acted upon. But this time, I was desperate.

That’s when I began to fall in love with my college, because every path that I diverged into over the course of the last three years was something that branched out from writing, and the only reason I became a writer is because of a very select group of people who loved me.

To demonstrate the affection of these five to seven individuals for me, I wrote over the span of three months, around 100+ blog entries which averaged 1k words each, and 10+ short stories different from each other. The blog entries were a daily feature for 2.5 months, ie., 80 days and these friends dedicatedly read every single one of them every day.

And to this day, when I write something new, inclusive of this very post, I personally send it to them when I publish it on Medium, a platform that I also discovered thanks to this college.

I also got to build a rock-solid online friendship with these people, the strangest assortment of folks with a bizarre taste in everything, like the flavours of personality catered to my tastes and to this day, they are the best thing that happened to me in this college, and possibly, in life.

That was the lockdown for me, the birth of a writer, the convergence of eccentric minds, and a brief cinephile phase where I watched around 20 films in 10 days.

---

The nurse attacked my nose with a needle and proclaimed me free of the virus. I could go meet my mom and dad after over a year. My nose still hurt, though.

College was back now, second year was ending, third year was beginning.

But I still felt like a first year, my immediate seniors felt like second years, and there was a whole new batch of kids in place.

The subjects were finally the branch I was studying, though.

Now I had branched out from writing. tried out UI/UX design in this lovely space this college affords for all interests, and I was liking it.

Motivated by a strange mixture of sheer stupidity and bravery, I registered for a two-month college-hosted online hackathon, despite never having written a single line of code, and the event being for 4-membered teams while I was solo.

My close friends, while confused at my choices, patted me on my back, “You got this”, and by this, they meant a lack of intelligence. Determined that if I could win the first prize here, it would mean I could do anything I set my mind to.

I worked for 11 days straight learning Flutter and what software dev was all about, mildly grasping Git, staying up till 6 AM and like, to the point my hand developed muscular pain rendering it of no further use.

The competition arrived.

I didn’t win, because this is the real world and I was a single person competing against 11 teams of 4 people each.


But I came in third.

That counted for something, I believed.

On the side, a delightful side effect of college going back offline, my friend circle had expanded now to include so many more exciting and affectionate people.

---

My friends were leaving.

Ninety percent of that select group of people I mentioned earlier were seniors I held dear to my heart, and they were going. This was the end of college as I knew it, despite there being one year left.

Who dictated that good things needed to come to an end? Because I definitely have a fight to pick with that person.

I sulked and frowned in _dukham,_ but what has to happen would happen.

No more new friends, and one way to work through the sorrow would be to throw myself into work regardless of where it came from. I could do well without idle time to laze around and mope in sadness.

So I went ahead and eventually took up positions in three different bodies in college, one informal, one formal, and one semi-formal, collecting them all like Pokemon, harnessing different aspects of myself.

No more sorrow, right?

Terrible miscalculation.

Of all the terribly marvelous things to happen, I made a whole new set of friends, people from my batch that I had never conversed with and adorable juniors that I fell in love with.

---

I am leaving my friends now.

And it just screams unfair all over again.

I didn’t have any more college work. I finished my last exam today.

I find myself today with some idle time to mope around now as well, a gap after an entire year of running away from what was inevitable.

I am nervous about what comes next, I know what I will be doing but I find myself again in an unfamiliar setting, and I get glimpses of that confused boy in his first year once more.

What’s next? Can I afford to make mistakes now?

Do I have to make friends all over again? And what does it mean if leaving them is inevitable, should I question the point of it? More importantly, do I have the energy to reinvent myself yet again after four years of the persona that I feel I played?

Am I good enough to survive out there?

Writing should help me deal with what is becoming what was, and where to go from here on out. So I flesh this all out because all of it seems important to me. Worthy and deserving of being recorded. The words escape me when I try to describe what I feel.

People tell me it’s okay.

I think that, if you have a reason to cry, that’s alright.

Does it mean that knowing that everything will be okay, you will meet new people, have new experiences, enter the largest ecosystem that is the modern world, and step into the next phase of life, means you shouldn’t mourn the death of what was?

No, I think you should and I will.

Que sera, sera.

Whatever will be, will be.

I take pride in the fact that I was shaped by the people this college led me to, the events that happened over the years in this satellite-inspired relic, into somebody that 12th-grade me in my farewell suit never imagined.

He would be jealous that I know so much more, did so much more, and have the best set of friends that a person could ask for, which may be where the sweet point of life is at right now.

And I am content.

![A new checkpoint.](https://miro.medium.com/v2/resize:fit:700/1*8sht3Z4mkjmM006VrplNcw.jpeg)

This college is a projection of the people who have walked its’ halls, a living unpredictable organism that’s shaped the trajectory of my life, the fluency of my words, the frames through which I view the world, the knowledge I grasped of the waking environment around me and I am thankful for all of it.

Farewell, MEC.
