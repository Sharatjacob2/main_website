My cousin testified yesterday that the first time I spoke to her about AI Safety, I did so for three hours, outlining lore, problems, work, personal stories, and she was hooked and spellbound the whole time. 

However, later, when she tried to recall the specifics of what I had discussed, she couldn’t remember a single item. Except this central idea that progress in developing advanced AI was bad and she had been presented with an overwhelming amount of evidence.

Oddly, this wasn’t the first time this had occurred to me.

In 2023, I was invited after my graduation to lecture a collection of first-years by my CS HOD to talk about progress in AI and AI Safety research. It was under the span of an hour, not practiced, and hastily delivered. 

Later, I found a way to learn about how the session went by asking a friend’s friend’s friend. The feedback was: “didn’t really grasp what he covered, but it was clear that it was pretty cool and important, and he seemed fun too.”

The first point I had expected, but the remaining, not so much.

The Nazi support and British fascist, Unity Mitford, once noted, as many did, how emotionally charged and powerful Adolf Hitler’s speeches were. They pulled at sentiments deep within.

But what I love was this anecdote by Mitford, I believe, that she once heard a speech by Adolf, found it powerful but she couldn’t really recall the contents or what he really spoke for. This came to mind when my cousin recounted my three-hour talk on AI Safety, because the elements of the situations appeared to share a common thread. (At this point, bringing the Nazi dictator into the equation, the cousin stared at me and said, “You are free to take from that what you will.”)

Out of a hundred people who take up drinking, over one-third of them are guaranteed to die due to liver disease. Light drinking, mind you, not heavy. It’s fixable, stoppable, but unfortunately, it’s one of the biggest evils that our society has co-opted as normal and in fact, even social, despite being a massive driver of human mortality.

Now, the crucial part is that everything in the previous paragraph isn’t true, but repeating certain ideas over and over are enough to hold truth-space in people’s minds. You can just say anything. As long as you say it with conviction, use the right literary devices, and pad it with that ‘oomph’ factor.

Famed leaders of industry and country often engaged in this, a notable example being the reality distortion field attributed to Steve Jobs. This term loosely refers to the mix of charismatic ability, hyperbole and bravado that Steve brought to convince the original Apple team to push boundaries, while also sometimes using the same skill for his own selfish ends, i.e, rewrite reality using his words. There’s a whole Wikipedia article on this idea, feel free to read, many other leaders have been praised/criticized for this same idea, including the current US president.

Interestingly, I think variants of this idea have been witnessed by many over the past few years, where reality is gradually amended bit by bit, that US TV anchors begin to argue that 14 year olds being targeted for pedophilia isn’t as bad as 9 year olds roughly a few weeks before the Epstein files began releasing.

Our collective perception of reality itself lends to gradual manipulation.

Harvard psychologist Ellen Langer will provide us with the best transition to the reality distortion field, with her acclaimed study on line-cutting. Langer once conducted a study on what reasons will people accept to let someone else cut a queue and tested it out with three different reasons: 

> Version 1 (request only): “Excuse me, I have 5 pages. May I use the Xerox machine?”  
> Version 2 (request with a real reason): “Excuse me, I have 5 pages. May I use the xerox machine, because I’m in a rush?”  
> Version 3 (request with a fake reason): “Excuse me, I have 5 pages. May I use the xerox machine, because I have to make copies?”

You’ll notice that Version 3 didn’t make much sense. Using the phrase “because I have to make copies” was a fairly useless reason for skipping the line. Everyone waiting at the photocopier needed to make copies. The phrase contained no new information, but the researcher was trying to use it to justify the favor anyway.

These two extracts were taken from James Clear’s blog post on the same study.

Surprisingly, this senseless reason performed well. When the researchers analyzed the data, they found the following.

> Version 1: 60 percent of people let the researcher skip the line.  
> Version 2: 94 percent of people let the researcher skip ahead in line.  
> Version 3: 93 percent of people let the researcher skip ahead in line.

It’s absolutely insane that Version 3 has any success rate alone, let alone the fact that it gives Version 2 a run for its’ money. Turns out compliance can be easy to elicit at times. To the point that reason flies out the window.

(Also turns out that there’s an amount of study conducted on line-cutting that one can google and read up about.)

In the West Wing, one of my favorite television shows famed for its dialogue by Aaron Sorkin, under the fictional President Jed Bartlett, his speechwriter Toby Ziegler goes on a rant about free trade. I invite you to not be interested in the content but in the portions about listener attention.



> Toby Ziegler: “You want to know the benefits of free trade? Food is cheaper.”  
> Officer Rhonda Sachs: “Yes.”  
> Toby Ziegler: “Food is cheaper! Clothes are cheaper. Steel is cheaper. Cars are cheaper. Phone service is cheaper. You feel me building a rhythm here? That’s because I’m a speech writer – I know how to make a point.”  
> Officer Rhonda Sachs: “Toby…”  
> Toby Ziegler: “It lowers prices, it raises income. You see what I did with ‘lowers’ and ‘raises’ there?”  
> Officer Rhonda Sachs: “Yes.”  
> Toby Ziegler: “It’s called the science of listener attention. We did repetition, we did floating opposites, and now you end with the one that’s not like the others. Ready? Free trade stops wars. Heh, and that’s it. Free trade stops wars! And we figure out a way to fix the rest. One world, one peace – I’m sure I’ve seen that on a sign somewhere.”  

Takes on free trade aside, I was really fascinated by Sorkin’s mention of the “science of listener attention”, something I couldn’t find other mentions of online. But I was hooked. I wanted to know more.

But it stands to make sense, literary devices aren’t merely an ancient mechanism that was reserved for Shakespeare. I strongly believe people are susceptible to well-scripted language to this day, lines selected for iconicity, and an order of information. (Sorkin himself deals with poetic meter at times, and his films with writer credit such as The Social Network, The Trial of the Chicago 7, The Newsroom, A Few Good Men amongst others.)

Another example in the West Wing, the dilemma of one episode is where they identify the current formala for poverty is flawed and needs to be revised, but it also means 4 million new Americans will be classified as living under poverty. But this is a problem for the administration because this will be used as a playing point by the opposition that the sitting president has increased poverty instead of decreasing it.

In the end, they meet the political campaign planner for the President, who shrugs and says, “Are you telling me the formula’s been broken for years and none of the other guys bothered to fix it?” which is his political solution to the issue.

Public perception is sometimes just words, which, if shuffled around, can reverse sentiment all on their own.

Random detour for the essay, since my life is occupied by AI Safety research, is that I think there’s a common trend to one of the markers we’ve noticed in our fatigue caused by AI-generated text. I am referring to “This is not just XYZ — it’s also ABC.”

Until the plethora of barely-prompted AI text flooded the Internet, this was actually a literary device that lends itself to gathering influence. Consider it. Any individual that starts out with this statement, and provides sufficient evidence to convince you, is engaging in a specific action. One that we connect to leaders.

They present the familiar, tilt your view of it and show you, the ignorant, something new and unfamiliar, an angle you never saw, a pattern you never noticed. They have engaged in enlightening you. Led you out of the darkness. How would you not feel admiration for such an individual?

Since AI has been trained on terabytes of some of the finest writing produced by the human race, it is barely a surprise that it picked up on this literary device and utilizes it freely to gain brownie points with a user.

This does generate discussion in future directions of how future AIs might resort to using other markers/literary devices to present convincing arguments.
